\begin{abstract}
  A non-defaced T1-weighted MRI contains two distinct aging signals in a
  single file: the brain parenchyma and the complete facial anatomy.
  We present an open pipeline that extracts both, running a brain-age model
  (SynthBA) and two face-age methods---multi-view surface renders scored by
  FaceAge, and morphometric features from BioFace3D-20 landmarks---in
  parallel on the same volume.
  %
  On the cross-sectional IXI dataset ($N{=}406$ face, $N{=}105$ brain,
  $N{=}93$ overlapping), SynthBA achieves MAE~$6.33$ yr ($r{=}0.84$) and
  face morphometrics achieve MAE~$7.81$ yr ($r{=}0.78$); the two aging gaps
  are positively correlated ($r{=}0.31$, $p{<}0.01$).
  %
  The SIMON single-subject dataset (99 scans, 36 scanners, age 29--46 yr)
  reveals a critical failure mode: SynthBA predicts a near-constant age of
  $27.4\,\pm\,1.26$ yr regardless of the subject's actual age
  (MAE~$16.2$ yr), demonstrating that high scanner reproducibility and
  severe out-of-distribution bias can coexist.
  Face morphometrics, by contrast, show a detectable positive trend with
  chronological age despite positive bias.
  %
  Together, these results highlight both the untapped information available
  in non-defaced MRI and the readiness gap of current models for
  out-of-distribution subjects.
\end{abstract}

\begin{keywords}

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Introduction}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

Standard MRI pre-processing pipelines routinely remove facial structure
(``defacing'') to protect participant identity before data sharing.
However, non-defaced T1-weighted volumes---common in single-site research
and legacy datasets such as IXI~\citep{ixi} and
SIMON~\citep{simon}---contain both a brain and a complete facial surface
within the same acquisition.  This raises a natural question: do the
\emph{facial} morphology and the \emph{brain parenchyma} record the same
aging trajectory, or do they carry partly independent biological information?

Prior work on brain age~\citep{peng2021sfcn, lemaitre2022synthba} and face
age~\citep{bontempi2025faceage} treats these as entirely separate modalities
from different instruments.  No existing study compares the two gap
estimates from a \emph{single scan}.  Such a comparison avoids the
domain shift of photo-vs-MRI studies and allows a controlled test of
whether the face and brain age together or drift apart.

We contribute: (1) an open pipeline that renders MRI-derived face images and
runs both modalities in parallel; (2) a systematic comparison of render
strategies; and (3) an analysis of face-age/brain-age gap correlation and
scanner reproducibility on two public datasets.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Methods}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

\paragraph{Face branch.}
Given a T1 NIfTI volume, we extract the iso-surface with marching cubes
(intensity threshold~$t{=}30$) and render a frontal portrait using
PyVista~\citep{pyvista} with nine viewpoints ($\pm 20°$ yaw/pitch) and
randomised lighting.  We apply FaceAge~\citep{bontempi2025faceage}, a
ResNet-50 model trained on 56\,304 curated photographs, to each render and
average the predictions.  We also tested photorealistic augmentation via
stable diffusion to close the photo--render domain gap, but found this
substantially worsened accuracy (see Table~\ref{tab:ixi}).

To correct the systematic positive bias of FaceAge on MRI renders, we
fit a linear calibration $\hat{y}_{\text{chron}} = a \cdot \hat{y}_{\text{face}} + b$
on a held-out set of 20 subjects and apply it to the remaining 80.

\paragraph{Brain branch.}
Skull stripping is performed with SynthStrip~\citep{synthstrip}.  We then
run SynthBA~\citep{lemaitre2022synthba}, a segmentation-based model robust
to acquisition variability, on the stripped volume.

\paragraph{Datasets.}
The \textbf{IXI} dataset~\citep{ixi} contains 561 T1 scans of healthy adults
(age 20--86 years) from three London sites.  After manual quality control of
frontal renders (removing scans with floating artefacts or missing facial
structure), 406 subjects were retained for face-age analysis; the brain-age
model was run on a 105-subject subset.  Both sets share 93 subjects, enabling
paired gap analysis.

The \textbf{SIMON} dataset~\citep{simon} provides 99 T1 scans of a single
healthy male (age range 29--46 years) collected across 73 sessions on 36
different scanners, enabling a direct test of scan-rescan reproducibility.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Results}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

\paragraph{IXI: proof-of-concept accuracy.}
On the IXI cross-sectional cohort ($N{=}406$ face, $N{=}105$ brain,
$N{=}93$ overlapping), the pipeline extracts detectable age signals from
both modalities of the same scan (Table~\ref{tab:ixi}).
The brain branch (SynthBA) outperforms all face variants.
Among face methods, morphometric features derived from BioFace3D-20
landmarks achieve the lowest MAE ($7.81$ yr) and are the most calibrated.
FaceAge applied to multi-view MRI renders shows a large systematic positive
bias: the model was trained on photographs, and MRI surface renders
constitute a clear out-of-distribution input.
Photorealistic augmentation via stable diffusion worsens this further,
suggesting the generated skin texture conflicts with, rather than bridges,
the domain gap.
On the 93 overlapping subjects the face-age gap and brain-age gap are
modestly but significantly correlated (Pearson $r{=}0.31$,
Spearman $\rho{=}0.32$, $p{<}0.01$).

\paragraph{SIMON: out-of-distribution stress test.}
The SIMON dataset provides a unique controlled experiment: one healthy male
subject scanned on 36 different scanners across 73 sessions (age at scan:
29.6--46.4 years).  Because there is only a single subject, any
\emph{between-scan} variance reflects scanner variability rather than
biology, and the model's ability to track the subject's \emph{longitudinal}
aging can be read directly from the slope of predictions over time.

Figure~\ref{fig:simon} plots chronological age against predicted age for
all three methods.  Three patterns stand out.

\textbf{SynthBA (brain)} produces a nearly flat response: mean predicted age
$27.4\,\pm\,1.26$ years across all 99 scans despite the subject aging from
$\approx30$ to $\approx46$ years.  Scanner-to-scanner reproducibility is
excellent (SD~$1.26$ yr), but absolute accuracy is poor
(MAE~$16.2$ yr, bias $-16.2$ yr).  The model is effectively frozen at a
value determined during training-distribution fitting and cannot track
longitudinal change in this subject.  This is a textbook out-of-distribution
failure: the model is highly confident and highly wrong.

\textbf{Face morphometrics} (GPA+EDMA features, Ridge regression) show a
positive bias of $+5.1$ yr and a scatter of SD~$5.6$ yr, but crucially
\emph{a visible positive trend} with chronological age, suggesting that the
3D morphometric representation captures some longitudinal aging signal even
without task-specific retraining.

\textbf{FaceAge multi-view renders} show the largest bias ($+8.5$ yr,
SD~$5.7$ yr) and the widest scatter, consistent with the domain gap between
MRI surface renders and the photographic training distribution.

\section{Discussion and Conclusion}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

A non-defaced T1 MRI volume contains two aging clocks in a single file:
the brain parenchyma and the facial anatomy.  Our pipeline demonstrates
that both can be read with existing open-source models, and that the
resulting aging gaps are modestly but significantly correlated ($r{=}0.31$).

The SIMON experiment makes the central limitation of the field visible.
State-of-the-art brain-age models are highly reproducible across hardware
but fail to generalise beyond the population distribution seen during
training: a healthy subject in his 30s--40s receives predictions anchored
near the model's training-set mean regardless of his actual age.
Face-based models face a different but equally severe domain shift: they
were trained on colour photographs, not MRI surface renders.
Together, these failures reveal that \emph{current models are not ready
for deployment outside their training distribution}---a finding that is
obscured when evaluation is limited to cross-sectional in-distribution
cohorts.

At the same time, the morphometric face branch---which encodes only
geometry, not colour or texture---shows a positive longitudinal trend
that the brain model entirely misses.  This suggests that T1 MRI facial
structure contains aging information complementary to brain parenchymal
signals, motivating the joint pipeline proposed here.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Several planned components were not completed in this work.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Additional brain-age models.}  SFCN~\citep{peng2021sfcn},
    MIDIBrainAge, and BrainIAC were integrated only partially; wrappers
    exist but age-bin decoding and full evaluation on IXI and SIMON
    remain in progress.

  \item \textbf{Multi-contrast face renders.}  The pipeline currently
    uses T1-only surface renders.  T2 and PD modalities available in IXI
    could add complementary tissue contrast to the face branch but have
    not been evaluated.

  \item \textbf{No longitudinal face-age reliability.}  Scanner
    reproducibility was assessed for the brain branch only.  The SD of
    face-age predictions across the 36 SIMON scanners was not formally
    compared to the brain-age SD.

  \item \textbf{No clinical or disease labels.}  Both IXI and SIMON
    contain healthy subjects.  Testing whether face--brain gap discordance
    carries diagnostic information (e.g.\ in neurodegeneration or
    inflammatory disease) requires a labelled clinical cohort not included
    here.

  \item \textbf{No per-subject calibration.}  The large subject-level bias
    of SynthBA on SIMON ($-16.2$ yr) calls for a per-subject or
    longitudinal recalibration strategy, which was not implemented.
\end{itemize}

Future work will address these gaps and extend the pipeline to datasets
with disease labels to test whether face--brain age discordance carries
diagnostic information beyond standard brain-age gap models.
